What is Kabaddi?

Kabaddi is a contact team sport with seven players per side, originating from South Asia, where a single player called a "raider" runs into the opposing half of the court to tag defenders and return safely within a single breath. The game involves strategy, strength, and agility, with points awarded for each tagged player, and defenders scoring by tackling the raider.

History and Origin of Kabaddi

The history of Kabaddi is rooted deeply in ancient India, with evidence suggesting its existence over 4,000 years ago. It is generally believed to have originated in the Indian subcontinent, specifically in the state of Tamil Nadu, where it was known by various local names like sadugudu or gudugudu. The game evolved as a form of self-defense training and military strategy, teaching quick reflexes, muscular strength, and holding one's breath (which was crucial for ancient warfare). Initially, Kabaddi was a sport played primarily at the village level and was integrated into cultural festivals and local gatherings. There were no standardized rules; local variations existed across different regions. It wasn't until the early 20th century that efforts began to formalize the game. The first set of codified rules for Kabaddi was established in Maharashtra, India, in 1921, to make it suitable for inter-village and regional competitions.

Evolution of the Sport

A major turning point in the sport's standardization came with the establishment of the All India Kabaddi Federation (AIKF) in 1950, which officially set the comprehensive rules and court dimensions that form the basis of the modern standard style. This formal structure helped propel Kabaddi onto the national stage. Furthermore, the sport gained international recognition when it was demonstrated at the 1936 Berlin Summer Olympics and later included as a medal sport in the 1990 Beijing Asian Games, marking its official entry into major global competitions. The launch of the professional Pro Kabaddi League (PKL) in 2014 revolutionized the sport, turning it into a high-octane, commercially successful spectacle played on mats, showcasing both athleticism and strategy.

The fundamental rules are simple, making the game easy to follow:

  • The Raid: A raider must cross the central line and chant "Kabaddi" repeatedly without taking a breath.
  • Scoring Points: Points are scored when the raider successfully tags a defender and returns to their half, or when the defenders successfully tackle the raider.
  • Out/Revival: A player is declared 'out' when they are tagged/tackled. They are 'revived' (brought back into the game) when their team scores a point.
  • Lona: If an entire team is put out, the opposing team scores 2 extra points, known as a 'Lona.'

Official Kabaddi Rules

A comprehensive overview of the Pro Kabaddi ruleset.

1. Playing Area & Teams

  • Field Dimensions: The Kabaddi court measures 13 meters by 10 meters for Men. (12m x 8m for Women).
  • Court Lines: The court includes a Mid-Line, Baulk Line (3.75m from the Mid-Line), and a Bonus Line (1m past the Baulk Line).
  • Teams: Each team consists of 12 players. 7 players are on the mat, and 5 are substitutes.
  • Match Duration: A match is divided into two halves of 20 minutes each, with a 5-minute break at halftime. Teams exchange sides after the break.

2. The Raid (Offensive Rules)

  • The Cant: The raider must chant "Kabaddi, Kabaddi" continuously and audibly without breaking breath throughout the entire raid. If the cant is broken or stopped, the raid ends, and the raider is declared out.
  • Crossing the Baulk Line: The raider must cross the Baulk Line into the opponent's court during the raid to validate it. If they return without crossing it, the raid is deemed unsuccessful and they are out (or a point is given to the defense).
  • Touch and Return: To score points, the raider must touch one or more defenders (or any part of their body/kit) and return to their half of the court with their cant intact.
  • The Bonus Line: If there are 6 or 7 defenders on the mat, the Bonus Line becomes active. If the raider crosses the Bonus Line with one foot while keeping the other foot in the air (without being tackled), they score 1 Bonus Point, even if they fail to score a touch point.

3. Defense, Scoring, and Penalties

  • Scoring Touch Points: The raiding team gets 1 point for every defender touched by the raider who returns successfully. The defender touched is then out.
  • Scoring Tackle Points: The defending team gets 1 point if they successfully hold or tackle the raider and prevent them from crossing the Mid-Line to return to their half. The raider is then out.
  • LONA (All Out): If a team manages to get all 7 opposing players out, they score a "LONA" and are awarded 2 extra points. All 7 players of the opposing team are immediately revived.
  • Revival: For every point scored by a team, one of their players who is "out" is revived in the order they were put out (first out, first in).
  • Infringements (Stalling): The raider must not deliberately stall or spend too much time in the opponent's court without attempting a touch or crossing the Baulk Line. The referee can declare the raid over or award a Technical Point to the defense for stalling.
  • 30-Second Raid Rule (Pro Kabaddi): Each raid has a maximum duration of 30 seconds. If the raid is not completed within this time, the raider is declared out, and the defending team gets a point.
  • Empty Raid: If a raider returns to their side without scoring any touch or bonus points, it is an Empty Raid. After three consecutive Empty Raids by one team, the third raider is declared out, and the defense gets a point (this is the "Do-or-Die" raid rule).

4. Substitutions & Timeouts

  • Substitutions: A maximum of 5 substitutions are allowed for each team during the match. They can be made during timeouts or the half-time break.
  • Timeouts: Each team is generally allowed a fixed number of Team Timeouts (e.g., 2 per match) and a Super Tackle Timeout (when only 3 or fewer defenders are on the mat).
  • Super Tackle: If the defending team successfully tackles the raider when they have 3 or fewer players on the mat, it is called a Super Tackle, and the defense scores 2 points instead of 1.
